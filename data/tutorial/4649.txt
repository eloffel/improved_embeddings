optimality theory 101: constraints > rules

   [30]aproposofamelate:

     okay, so here   s a crash course in optimality theory (or ot) for any
     confused linguistics or curious parties out there.

     at it   s base, ot is essentially just an alternative way to view
     phonology. instead of rules to figure out what is and is not
        allowed    in a language ot uses constraints and structures grammars
     as systems that map from the input to the output. the input is
     referred to the as underlying form whereas the output is the surface
     realization.

     [31]read more

   another entry in [32]crowdsourced linguistics! yay!

   sometimes people also use non-linguistics decision-making analogies to
   explain optimality theory: [33]here   s a coffee-buying analogy
   via [34]linguisticky, for example. i just realized that this analogy
   doesn   t link into the more formal layout of ot, with the tableaux and
   such, so i   m going to do that below.

   let   s say you   re trying to decide how to get coffee (input). for the
   sake of this example, you   ve got six options for how to get it (gen):
   don   t bother at all, make terrible instant coffee, brew your own really
   good coffee from scratch, get a crappy cup at the nearby corner store,
   get a pretty good coffee from slightly-further-away starbucks, or get a
   really good but expensive coffee from an indie coffeeshop at quite a
   distance away.

   you   ve also got four relevant constraints (con): you want caffeine
   (we   ll ignore tea and other sources for the sake of simplicity), you
   want it to be easy and taste good, and you don   t want it to be
   expensive.

   if we put the candidates and the constraints in a tableau, with the
   constraints ranked in their importance to you from left to right, we
   can figure out where you should get your coffee (this whole step is
   known as evaluation or eval).

   as soon as a candidate violates a constraint, we put in an asterisk, or
   multiple asterisks if it violates it several times (to account for the
   fact that instant tastes worse than corner store which tastes worse
   than starbucks, for example). the violation that results in a candidate
   being    kicked out    of the running compared to the remaining candidates
   is a fatal violation, and gets marked with an exclamation mark. once
   we   ve put in all the necessary asterisks and exclamation marks, we can
   look for the row without any fatal violations. since it didn   t get
   kicked out, that row must represent the least bad (=optimal) candidate,
   and we indicate it with a pointing hand.

   for example, if my constraint ranking is the following, the most
   optimal candidate is to buy my coffee at the corner store.

   image

   however, if i actually care more about how good my coffee tastes than
   about its price or ease of making it, then tastes-good is ranked
   higher, and i end up with a different option: making my own brewed
   coffee.

   image

   in practice, different people have different constraint rankings, which
   explains how all these different coffee-selling options are able to
   remain in business, but we all care about caffeine, taste, price, and
   ease to at least some degree (even a millionaire would balk at a
   billion-dollar cup, or even the least snobbish drinker wouldn   t take a
   cup that actually tasted like dirt). similarly, the idea in linguistics
   is that languages all have the same set of constraints but have
   different (perhaps drastically different) constraint rankings, which
   explains the interplay between language-general trends and
   language-specific differences.

   you can already see a few issues when it comes to doing ot outside of
   constructed examples: how do you know which and how many candidates to
   generate, for example? in theory, the possible candidates is infinite
   (which yeah, creates some issues for how our brain computes through
   them all), but some of them are so obviously terrible that in practice
   we don   t consider them, such as travelling halfway around the world to
   get a cup of terrible and expensive coffee or having an input /mama/
   create an output /psmordlnf/.

   another thing to consider about ot is where the constraints come from.
   the notion of cross-linguistic constraints captures really nicely the
   intuitive notion that certain sequences are just easier or more natural
   than others in any language: realistically, /mama/ is easier for anyone
   than /psmordlnf/ (and one is more likely to be a baby   s first word,
   regardless of input). so probably languages have some sort of
   constraint in favour of cv syllables. in languages like hawaiian, where
   there aren   t any non-cv syllables at all, this constraint is very
   highly ranked, while in languages like english or russian, where there
   are lots of non-cv syllables, this constraint is very low (but not
   completely absent: nicknames, for example, tend to be cv or cvc. a
   constraint showing up in edge cases like this is known as the
   [35]emergence of the unmarked). but that does mean that if you   re
   proposing a constraint for one phenomenon in one language, you   re also
   hypothetically proposing it for all languages ever, which is part of
   what makes really good ot quite hard.
